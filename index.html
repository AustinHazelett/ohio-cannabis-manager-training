DCC Compliance & Ohio Regulations

Master the regulatory framework that governs every aspect of your dispensary's operation.

ℹ️
Regulatory Authority The Ohio Division of Cannabis Control (DCC), housed within the Ohio Department of Commerce, is the single regulatory authority for both medical and adult-use cannabis in Ohio — handling all licensing, compliance enforcement, and rulemaking.
⚖️ Senate Bill 56 — What Changed in 2026 ▼
Effective March 20, 2026

Ohio SB 56, signed December 19, 2025, represents the most significant overhaul of Ohio's cannabis regulatory framework since legalization. Key changes managers must know:

  • Statewide dispensary cap set at 400 licenses. No new licenses can be issued beyond this total.
  • Single ownership cap. Individual owners may hold no more than 8 dispensary licenses, 1 cultivator license, and 1 processor license.
  • DCC becomes sole authority. The DCC now handles both medical and adult-use regulation under one unified framework.
  • Updated purchase limits. Medical daily limits were revised effective March 24, 2026 (see purchase limits section).
  • Source restriction upheld. Dispensaries may only sell marijuana obtained from a DCC-licensed cultivator or processor.
🛍️ Purchase Limits — Adult-Use vs. Medical ▼

🧑 Adult-Use (Recreational) — 21+

  • 2.5 oz (70.75g) flower per day Updated June 2025
  • 15,000 mg total THC in processed forms per day
  • Edibles: max 110 mg THC per package
  • Max 10 mg THC per individual serving
  • Subject to 10% excise tax

🏥 Medical Patients — 18+

  • Up to 10 oz/day (with some limitations) Updated March 2026
  • Up to 2.5 oz/day with no limitations
  • Edibles: up to 1,500 mg / 55 mg per serving
  • High-dose edibles reserved for medical patients only
  • Exempt from 10% excise tax
🚫
High-Dose Edibles — Medical Only As of 2025 rule updates, adult-use consumers no longer have access to high-dose THC edibles. Products exceeding 110 mg per package are RESTRICTED to medical cardholders only. Selling high-dose edibles to adult-use customers is a compliance violation.
🪪 ID Verification Requirements ▼

ID verification is your first line of compliance defense. Every sale must begin with a proper ID check.

  • Check a valid, government-issued photo ID for every customer, every visit — no exceptions for regulars.
  • Adult-use: customer must be 21 or older. Medical: patient must be 18 or older with an active Ohio MMJ card.
  • Acceptable IDs: state driver's license, state ID, US passport, military ID. All must be unexpired.
  • Verify the medical patient's Ohio MMJ card is active and not expired before any medical sale.
  • Document refusals — if a customer cannot provide valid ID, refuse the sale and log the attempt.
  • Use your POS system's ID scanning feature to record age verification and flag expired documents.
⚠️
Training Your Staff All budtenders must be trained on ID verification procedures. As manager, you are responsible for ensuring every team member can identify valid vs. invalid IDs, recognize altered documents, and handle confrontational situations appropriately.

Metrc / Seed-to-Sale Tracking

Ohio's official statewide track-and-trace system — every gram must be accounted for in real time.

ℹ️
What is Metrc? Metrc is Ohio's mandatory seed-to-sale tracking system, in use since 2018. It tracks all cannabis as it moves from cultivators through processors to dispensaries and finally to customers. The DCC monitors your Metrc data in real time.
🌱 Core Metrc Concepts for Dispensary Managers ▼

Key Metrc Objects

  • Packages — Tagged units of cannabis with a unique Metrc ID (RFID tag)
  • Transfers — Recorded movement of packages between licensees
  • Adjustments — Corrections to package quantities with approved reasons
  • Sales — Each retail transaction recorded in Metrc via your POS
  • Plants — Not applicable at retail; cultivator-level tracking

Manager Responsibilities

  • Ensure all incoming inventory is received and accepted in Metrc on arrival
  • Ensure POS syncs sales data to Metrc in real time or per session
  • Review Metrc inventory vs. physical count in weekly audits
  • Authorize and document all package adjustments
  • Ensure Metrc data is current before any DCC inspection
📥 Receiving Inventory — Step-by-Step ▼
  • Verify that the incoming transfer manifest is active and visible in your Metrc account before accepting any product.
  • Physically count and inspect all packages. Check that Metrc RFID tags are intact and match the manifest quantities.
  • Verify the vendor holds a current, valid DCC license — confirm via the DCC's online licensee search if unfamiliar.
  • Accept the transfer in Metrc promptly. Do not leave transfers in "In Transit" status. The DCC monitors outstanding transfers.
  • Flag and document any discrepancies (wrong quantities, damaged products, missing tags) before accepting.
  • Move received packages into appropriate inventory locations in Metrc (e.g., vault/sales floor) and your POS system.
  • Retain the transfer manifest (physical or electronic) as part of your 3-year record-keeping obligation.
🔧 Package Adjustments — October 2025 Guidance ▼
Updated: October 22, 2025

The DCC issued updated guidance on package adjustments in October 2025. Adjustments must be made promptly to keep Metrc accurate. The DCC actively monitors adjustment patterns.

🖥️ POS Integration & Sales Recording ▼

Your Point of Sale (POS) system should integrate directly with Metrc. Understanding how this works prevents compliance gaps.

  • Configure your POS to sync sales to Metrc automatically at time of sale or end of business day (real-time preferred).
  • Each sale must include: customer ID verification confirmation, items sold with Metrc package IDs, quantities, and sale totals.
  • POS must enforce daily purchase limits — the system should prevent overselling to a single customer.
  • Ensure your Metrc API key is current. Expired API keys break the POS-Metrc connection — check monthly.
  • Reconcile POS records against Metrc at end of each business day to catch any sync failures immediately.
  • Maintain POS transaction records as backup documentation alongside Metrc data.
✅
Best Practice Designate a "Metrc Lead" on each shift — a staff member responsible for monitoring the Metrc dashboard for any red flags (pending transfers, sync errors) throughout the day.
📊 Inventory Reconciliation & Audits ▼

Ohio rules require dispensaries to conduct and document a formal inventory audit at least once weekly, with an annual audit compiled by a CPA or auditor.

  • Count all physical cannabis products on hand — flower, concentrates, edibles, topicals, tinctures, accessories.
  • Compare physical counts to Metrc package balances. Document any discrepancies with explanations.
  • Investigate discrepancies promptly. Determine whether the cause is a Metrc error, POS sync issue, or actual product loss.
  • Make approved Metrc adjustments for explainable discrepancies. Unexplained losses require DCC notification within 2 business days.
  • Document the audit results: date, auditor name, findings, and corrective actions taken. Retain for 3+ years.
  • For the annual audit, engage a licensed CPA or auditor to compile and certify the annual inventory report.

Product Segregation Medical-only products (high-dose edibles, higher-limit inventory) must be clearly labeled and separated from adult-use inventory on your sales floor. Budtenders must understand which products are available to which customer types.
🌟 Sales Floor Best Practices ▼

Before the Sale

  • Greet every customer professionally and promptly
  • Check ID at the entry point and again at the register
  • Confirm medical card validity (for medical patients)
  • Ask about the customer's needs/goals before recommending products

During the Sale

  • Educate customers on products objectively — avoid health claims
  • Use POS to verify customer hasn't hit daily limits
  • Confirm correct product tier (medical vs. adult-use)
  • Review the transaction total with customer before checkout
🚫
Prohibited Sales Practices Staff may NOT make medical claims about cannabis products. Do not tell customers that cannabis will "treat," "cure," or "diagnose" any condition. Stick to product attributes (terpenes, potency, effects profiles) and encourage customers to consult healthcare providers for medical advice.
📚 Product Education for Your Team ▼

Well-educated budtenders build customer trust and reduce compliance risk. Managers should conduct regular product training sessions.

Product Categories to Know

  • Flower — Indica, Sativa, Hybrid; cannabinoid and terpene profiles
  • Concentrates — Wax, shatter, live resin, distillate; methods of consumption
  • Edibles — Onset times, dosing guidance, medical vs. adult-use limits
  • Tinctures / Capsules — Sublingual vs. ingested absorption
  • Topicals — Non-intoxicating; local vs. transdermal
  • Vapes / Cartridges — Hardware types; battery compatibility

Budtender Training Topics

  • THC vs. CBD vs. CBN vs. CBG — effects and use cases
  • Terpene education (myrcene, limonene, pinene, etc.)
  • Onset times by consumption method
  • New customer dosing guidance — "start low, go slow"
  • Contraindications to mention (consult doctor if on medications)
  • Ohio's labeling requirements and how to read a product label

👥 Managing Your Budtender Team ▼
  • Ensure all budtenders complete initial DCC compliance orientation before their first customer interaction.
  • Conduct regular refresher trainings — at minimum quarterly — covering regulation updates, product knowledge, and customer service.
  • Implement a "mystery shop" or observed sale program quarterly to assess compliance adherence on the floor.
  • Maintain documentation of all staff training — dates, topics covered, and employee signatures. The DCC may request this during inspections.
  • Establish clear escalation procedures — staff must know when to call you (the manager) vs. handle independently.
  • Review the quality assurance log monthly — per DCC rules, the Responsible Party must review all dispensing errors at least once per calendar month.

SOPs & Daily Operations

Standardized procedures keep your dispensary running smoothly, your team aligned, and your compliance airtight.

☀️ Opening Procedures ▼
  • Disarm security system. Check overnight security camera logs for any alerts or incidents.
  • Count the opening cash drawer and verify it matches the prior closing count. Document any discrepancies.
  • Power on POS terminals and verify Metrc sync is active and connected. Check for any pending transfers in Metrc.
  • Complete the opening physical inventory spot-check — verify a sample of high-value items match Metrc balances.
  • Check product expiration dates and remove any expired or compromised products from the sales floor.
  • Confirm all required licenses and postings are visibly displayed (dispensary license, required DCC postings).
  • Brief the opening team: daily promotions, any product arrivals, compliance reminders, staffing assignments.
  • Unlock the entrance and open for business.
🌙 Closing Procedures ▼
  • Complete final customer transactions and close the register to new sales.
  • Reconcile POS sales totals against Metrc. Resolve any sync errors before closing.
  • Count all cash in registers. Reconcile against sales totals. Document cash count with two-person verification.
  • Secure cash in the safe following your dual-control cash handling SOP.
  • Move all remaining product from the sales floor back to the secure storage vault.
  • Conduct the end-of-day inventory spot-check and document results.
  • Ensure all Metrc data is current — no pending transfers, all sales recorded.
  • Verify electronic records are backed up as required by DCC rules.
  • Arm security system and perform perimeter check before leaving.
💵 Cash Handling & Financial Controls ▼
ℹ️
Many cannabis businesses remain cash-heavy due to federal banking restrictions. Proper cash controls protect your business and demonstrate compliance.

Cash Control Best Practices

  • Always use two-person verification for cash counts
  • Never leave cash unattended or unsecured
  • Maintain a clear chain of custody for all cash movements
  • Separate cash handling duties — the person counting should not be the same person recording
  • Conduct surprise cash audits regularly

Documentation Requirements

  • Log every cash count with date, time, amount, and employee signature
  • Document every safe access with employee name and timestamp
  • Retain cash reconciliation records as part of your 3-year record-keeping requirement
  • Report and investigate any cash discrepancies immediately
  • Maintain records available for DCC inspection

Incident Response Protocol Post emergency contacts (DCC, local police, your legal counsel) in a visible location in the manager's office. When in doubt — report it. The DCC views timely self-reporting favorably compared to violations discovered during inspections.
📅 Staff Scheduling & Coverage ▼

Scheduling Considerations

  • Always schedule a licensed Responsible Party (RP) or trained manager on every shift
  • Ensure at least two staff members are on during all open hours for security and dual-control purposes
  • Plan coverage for high-traffic hours (evenings, weekends, payday weeks)
  • Cross-train staff so no single point of failure exists for Metrc operations
  • Keep an on-call list for unexpected absences

Ongoing Training Calendar

  • Weekly: Brief team on any new products, Metrc updates, or compliance reminders
  • Monthly: QA review of dispensing errors with team
  • Quarterly: Full compliance refresher training; product knowledge update
  • Annually: Complete training documentation audit; update all SOPs
